Redesigning the Department of Labor (DOL) Website Navigation

The core focus of the project was to redesign the Department of Labor's website to address user challenges in efficiently finding the resources they need. These challenges included a crowded and elongated menu that made it cumbersome for users to access items located below the fold. My contributions in defining user flows, conducting card sorting exercises, and participating in sitemap development were instrumental in improving user navigation and content accessibility. We prioritized the menu navigation, recognizing it as the primary point of interaction for users. Through card sorting and usability testing we were able to simplfy the navigation menu and tailor it to the different audiences that would most frequently visit the page.

What

The DOL's mission is to foster, promote, and develop the welfare of the wage earners, job seekers, and retirees of the United States; improve working conditions; advance opportunities for profitable employment; and assure work-related benefits and rights.

Why

The project centered on the redesign of the Department of Labor (DOL) website, with a primary focus on enhancing the navigation menu. The DOL website contained a vast amount of valuable information, but the existing navigation presented challenges for users. Specifically, the navigation menu extended below the fold, making it challenging for users to access critical content. Furthermore, there was a lack of clear content grouping and prioritization, beyond broad categories like "Topics" and "Agencies." This project aimed to address these usability issues and provide an improved user experience for visitors to the DOL website.

User Research

In the early stages of this project, we conducted user interviews using the original site as a reference point to gain insights into the user experience. Our primary user persona, Rosemary, was the central figure in our design process. Her specific needs included easy access to information about SPA events, the ability to sign up her class or school to participate in the parade, insight into budget allocation, and a desire to engage with the organization. Additionally, we conducted a heuristic evaluation to identify usability issues and design flaws within the existing website. A key contribution I made was leading a competitive analysis, which, along with insights from low-fidelity usability tests, allowed us to prioritize updates to align with both stakeholder and user needs. Throughout the project, Rosemary's unique needs and pain points guided our design decisions, ensuring that the redesigned website effectively addressed critical issues and catered to the preferences of users like her.

Definition & Synthesis

Our user research process was comprehensive and focused on enhancing the DOL website's user experience. Key components included:

Proto-Persona: We developed a proto-persona to gain a better understanding of our target audience. This proto-persona represented an archetype of our typical website user, allowing us to design with their needs in mind.

User Paths Definition: We defined clear user paths, which were the optimal routes for users to access information. These paths served as the basis for improving navigation and content accessibility.

Usability Testing: To validate our design decisions and user paths, we conducted usability tests. This involved observing real users interacting with the website to identify pain points and areas for improvement.

Quadrant Analysis: After conducting usability tests, we created a quadrant analysis, plotting improvements based on two dimensions: user importance (X-axis) and DOL importance (Y-axis). This approach allowed us to prioritize enhancements, focusing on changes that mattered most to users and aligned with the DOL's objectives.

This user research process provided valuable insights and data-driven guidance for our redesign efforts, ensuring that the changes made were both user-centric and aligned with the DOL's mission.

Ideation

In the absence of site analytics and direct collaboration with the DOL (as this was a class assignment), our ideation process primarily relied on our own assessments and the usability tests we conducted. To better understand our users' needs and preferences, we employed the following methods:

User-Centric Exploration: We conducted user interviews, surveys, and usability tests to gain insights into user preferences and pain points. These activities allowed us to understand how users interacted with the website, despite the absence of site analytics.

Card Sorting and Information Architecture: Through card sorting exercises, we probed how users naturally grouped and categorized content. This exercise was pivotal in shaping the information architecture and optimizing the menu navigation, based on the information we had at hand.

Content Prioritization: In our ideation process, we deliberated the separation of content for employers and non-employers, including workers and the unemployed. Although we didn't have direct access to site analytics, we prioritized content organization based on our own assessment and understanding of user importance and relevance.

Navigation Styles: We also explored different navigation styles, specifically debating between side navigation and traditional top navigation. Our choices were guided by what we perceived as user-friendly, taking into account our usability test findings.

Prototyping

Our prototyping process began with user flow creation and low-fidelity digital prototyping, progressing into high-fidelity prototyping. We focused on enhancing menu button names for clarity and developed a visual design system for a cohesive user interface.

We created a user flow based on the results from our user flow tests, aiming to ensure a logical and efficient path for users to access information on the DOL website. We then transitioned to a low-fidelity digital prototype. This prototype was used in usability tests to assess the effectiveness of our navigation and menu button names. We made adjustments to enhance clarity and usability based on user feedback.

We developed a visual design system inspired by a mood board for styling, ensuring a cohesive and visually appealing user interface. Using our style guide we developed a hi-fi prototype, providing a visual representation of our proposed changes to the DOL website.
